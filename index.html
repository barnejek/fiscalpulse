Methodology
❄ The Snowball Effect (r − g)

The r − g spread measures the difference between the effective interest rate on public debt (r) and the nominal GDP growth rate (g). It is the central concept in debt sustainability analysis.

When r > g, the economy grows more slowly than debt costs accrue — even a balanced primary budget leads to a rising debt-to-GDP ratio. The debt "snowballs" automatically, requiring ever-larger primary surpluses just to stabilise it.

When r < g, nominal GDP growth "inflates away" the debt burden — the government can run moderate primary deficits while still keeping debt on a stable or declining path. This was the dominant regime in advanced economies for much of 2015–2021.

Debt(t+1) = Debt(t) × (1 + r) / (1 + g) − Primary Balance

⚡ Stress Test Scenarios
Geopolitical Shock

Sustained rise in energy commodity prices and global trade disruption. Models the fiscal impact of a supply-side shock similar to 2022: lower growth (−1.5 pp/yr), higher borrowing costs (+1.0 pp), and deteriorating primary balances (−0.5 pp/yr) due to emergency spending.

Market Repricing

A sharp correction in overvalued financial markets (e.g. tech sector), triggering a global financial stress event. Tax revenues from capital gains fall, funding costs spike sharply (+2.0 pp), and growth contracts (−1.0 pp/yr) as credit conditions tighten.

US Spillover

Erosion of the USD safe-haven premium under pressure from surging US debt supply. Rising US Treasury yields drag up global borrowing costs (+0.8 pp) regardless of local fiscal discipline — a transmission shock that punishes all sovereign issuers indiscriminately.
